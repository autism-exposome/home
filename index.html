Welcome to Autism Exposome Project


Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) is a neurodevelopmental disorder with unknown etiology in 99% of cases. Over the past decade, the prevalence of ASD has increased at an alarming rate in the U.S., while in the U.K. the prevalence has been relatively stable in the past decade after a five-fold increase in the 1990s. Younger age at diagnosis only accounts for 12% of reported increases in prevalence, and inclusive diagnosis alone – a 56% increase – cannot explain the increasing rate during the past decade. What is causing the increase in prevalence of ASD in the US?

  • Genetic factors

    • Genetic factors have been considered to play a significant role in susceptibility to ASD. However, large-scale screens for rare de novo mutations and copy number variations found that such variants explain less than 10% of ASD cases, cumulatively. Recently, twin studies revisited the heritability of the disease (the proportion of variability of disease attributed to genetic factors) and recognized that shared environmental factors can explain a larger proportion of the variance in liability (41~52%) relative to heritability (38~49%).

  • Environmental risk factors

    • Previous studies have identified possible exposure to individual or mixtures of environmental toxicants during different life preriods, such as: the comprehensively and systematically sesarch and identification of environmental exposures in ASD have not kept pace.



Autism Exposome Project aims to investigate what contributed a significant increase in the autism prevalence of the U.S., focusing on discovery of internal and external environmental exposures that are detectable in the blood from the patients with autism and their mothers, and the role these exposures play in gene function in autism.
